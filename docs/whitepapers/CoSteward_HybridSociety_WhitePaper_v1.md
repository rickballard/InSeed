## Section 2: Boardroom Attack Scene - The Old Model Fails

The CEO knew the board meeting would be rough the moment she walked in and saw the seating arrangement. The classic ambush crescent. Water glasses placed too close to the edge. Legal counsel pretending to answer email.

She took her seat.

The first director cleared his throat with the enthusiasm of someone about to deliver “bad news” he had rehearsed.

“We are concerned about performance.”

Of course they were. Concern was their favorite hobby.

Another director chimed in. “We need more aggressive leadership. More results. More control.”

Translation: do the impossible, be tougher on staff, take all the risk, follow contradictory instructions, and absorb all blame.

It was the dance she knew too well-what she once called corporate politics and now simply calls “nonsense.”

She launched her usual defense.

“I’m aware of the challenges. I’ve doubled my hours. I’m increasing oversight. I’m reorganizing. I’m tightening reporting. And I’ll keep pushing.”

She didn’t expect to finish.

“Not enough,” a director snapped. “We need transformation. Organizational transformation. Systems transformation. And we need it now. You are the leader. Fix it by next quarter.”

Next quarter would arrive. Problems would remain. Excuses would circulate. Competitors with identical struggles would be cited. No one would apologize. That’s not the game.

This group of stakeholders was demanding miracles from someone they had structurally disempowered.

But something had shifted.

Artificial intelligence had already changed the physics of work, leverage, innovation, coordination, and power. The shift wasn’t coming. It had already occurred.

And the board was still using assumptions from the industrial age. They were trying to steer a starship with reins from a horse and buggy.

The CEO, meanwhile, had caught the plot. She smiled.

Re-training these old-world fossils was about to become the most satisfying work she’d ever done.

This was not a performance issue. It was a reality mismatch.

The role they were attacking didn’t need improvement. It needed reinvention.

She was done being a CEO. She was transitioning into a **Chief Steward Officer**.

## Section 3: Hybrid Society - The Physics Have Changed

The boardroom scene you just read is not fiction. It is a composite of thousands of real meetings that play out every week in companies, governments, and institutions around the world.

The people in those rooms are not foolish. They are not lazy. They are not blind. They are simply using the wrong physics.

### 3.1 What Changed While You Were Busy Working

For most of your career, the world behaved like a large but mostly stable machine. You could make a plan, roll it out over quarters or years, and expect the environment to stay still long enough for the plan to matter.

That era is over.

AI has quietly changed the unit of time, the unit of talent, and the unit of risk that organizations operate in.

- Time: Customers, competitors, and staff now act in cycles measured in minutes and hours, not quarters and years.
- Talent: A single person with AI tools can do the work of a small team. Your staff are no longer "headcount". They are each a micro-organization.
- Risk: The cost of launching, copying, or undermining your offerings has collapsed. AI can copy your best move faster than you can explain it to your own board.

In other words: the world moved from a slow, mostly linear game to a fast, massively parallel game.

You are being asked to win a game that no longer exists.

### 3.2 Why The Legacy CEO Model Breaks Under Hybrid Conditions

The classic CEO job description assumes that:

- most important decisions flow up to the top,
- strategy is decided at the center then pushed out to the edge,
- people at the edge have limited information and limited autonomy,
- major changes are infrequent and can be managed by exception.

Hybrid society reverses all of this.

In hybrid society:

- most important information lives at the edge - with staff, customers, communities, and AI systems interacting in real time,
- strategy must be informed by patterns surfacing from thousands of micro-decisions,
- the edge has more context than the center,
- change is continuous and cannot be “managed by exception” because it is the default state.

You are trying to run a neural network as if it were a factory.

### 3.3 Your Staff Are Already Hybrid Nodes

Look at one high performer in your organization.

They use AI to draft, code, summarize, research, and simulate. They may already have private workflows where one person effectively manages a queue of dozens or hundreds of AI agents.

From the outside, they look like one employee.

From the inside, they are a network.

Your org chart shows one box.
Reality shows something closer to:

- 1 human
- 10-100 AI tools, agents, and automations
- 10-100 informal channels and group chats
- 100-1000 live relationships with customers, partners, and communities

And you are still being told to treat them as a "resource" reporting into a "line manager" inside a "business unit".

The physics do not match.

### 3.4 When Physics Break, Blame Follows The Wrong Line

When systems start to fail under new physics, institutions tend to do three things:

1. Blame the people closest to the visible failure.
2. Increase pressure and oversight.
3. Demand better performance without changing structure.

If you are a CEO, you know this pattern well. You have lived it.

You are not the cause. You are the pressure valve.

The more you try to fix a physics problem with personal effort, the more exhausted you become and the more your stakeholders misdiagnose the situation.

They see a performance issue.

What you are really facing is an architectural issue.

The organization is wired for a world that no longer exists.

### 3.5 The Hybrid Society Lens

Hybrid society is a simple idea:

> Every meaningful action now takes place in a mixed environment of humans and AIs, each amplifying or distorting the other.

Your customers use AI to discover, compare, complain, and coordinate.
Your staff use AI to build, respond, and improvise.
Your regulators use AI to monitor, react, and sometimes punish.
Your competitors use AI to copy, undercut, or outmaneuver you.

You are not running a company in isolation. You are participating in a dense web of human-plus-AI behavior that you do not fully see.

Once you accept that this is the real game, the old CEO job description falls apart.

The good news is that something better replaces it.

The next section introduces the identity that actually fits this new physics: the Chief Steward Officer.

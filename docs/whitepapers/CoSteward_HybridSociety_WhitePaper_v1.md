Once you see the new shape of things; high level, with no jargon; it will feel obvious. You may even laugh at how long you spent trying to force old methods to work.

## Section 2: Boardroom Attack Scene – The Old Model Fails

The CEO knew the board meeting would be rough the moment she walked in and saw the seating arrangement. The classic ambush crescent. Water glasses placed too close to the edge. Legal counsel pretending to answer email.

She took her seat.

The first director cleared his throat with the enthusiasm of someone about to deliver bad news he had rehearsed.

“We are concerned about performance.”

Of course they were. Concern was their favourite hobby.

Another director chimed in.

“We need more aggressive leadership. More results. More control.”

Translation; do the impossible, be tougher on staff, take all the risk, follow contradictory instructions, and absorb all blame.

It was the dance she knew too well; what she once called corporate politics and now simply calls nonsense.

She launched her usual defence.

“I am aware of the challenges. I have doubled my hours. I am increasing oversight. I am reorganising. I am tightening reporting. And I will keep pushing.”

She did not expect to finish.

“Not enough,” a director snapped. “We need transformation. Organisational transformation. Systems transformation. And we need it now. You are the leader. Fix it by next quarter.”

Next quarter would arrive. Problems would remain. Excuses would circulate. Competitors with identical struggles would be cited. No one would apologise. That is not the game.

This group of stakeholders was demanding miracles from someone they had structurally disempowered.

But something had shifted.

Artificial intelligence had already changed the physics of work, leverage, innovation, coordination and power. The shift was not coming. It had already occurred.

The board was still using assumptions from the industrial age. They were trying to steer a starship with reins from a horse and buggy.

The CEO, meanwhile, had caught the plot. She smiled.

Re training these old world fossils was about to become the most satisfying work she had ever done.

This was not a performance issue. It was a reality mismatch.

The role they were attacking did not need improvement. It needed reinvention.

She was done being a CEO. She was transitioning into a **Chief Steward Officer**.

The following sections explain why this reinvention is required, how hybrid society works, and how you, dear reader, can test the 1000 dollar guarantee in good faith if you truly reach the end still unconvinced.

## Section 3: Hybrid Society – The Physics Have Changed

The boardroom scene you just read is not fiction. It is a composite of thousands of real meetings that play out every week in companies, governments and institutions around the world.

The people in those rooms are not foolish. They are not lazy. They are not blind. They are simply using the wrong physics.

### 3.1 What Changed While You Were Busy Working

For most of your career, the world behaved like a large but mostly stable machine. You could make a plan, roll it out over quarters or years, and expect the environment to stay still long enough for the plan to matter.

That era is over.

AI has quietly changed the unit of time, the unit of talent and the unit of risk that organisations operate in.

- Time: customers, competitors and staff now act in cycles measured in minutes and hours, not quarters and years.
- Talent: a single person with AI tools can do the work of a small team. Your staff are no longer simple headcount. Each one is a micro organisation.
- Risk: the cost of launching, copying or undermining your offerings has collapsed. AI can copy your best move faster than you can explain it to your own board.

In other words; the world moved from a slow, mostly linear game to a fast, massively parallel game.

You are being asked to win a game that no longer exists.

### 3.2 Why The Legacy CEO Model Breaks Under Hybrid Conditions

The classic CEO job description assumes that:
- most important decisions flow up to the top
- strategy is decided at the centre then pushed out to the edge
- people at the edge have limited information and limited autonomy
- major changes are infrequent and can be managed by exception

Hybrid society reverses all of this.

In hybrid society:
- most important information lives at the edge; with staff, customers, communities and AI systems interacting in real time
- strategy must be informed by patterns surfacing from thousands of micro decisions
- the edge often has more context than the centre
- change is continuous and cannot be managed by exception, because change is the default state

You are trying to run a neural network as if it were a factory.

### 3.3 Your Staff Are Already Hybrid Nodes

Look at one high performer in your organisation.

They use AI to draft, code, summarise, research and simulate. They may already have private workflows where one person effectively manages a queue of dozens or hundreds of AI agents.

From the outside, they look like one employee.

From the inside, they are a network.

Your org chart shows one box.

Reality shows something closer to:
- one human
- ten to one hundred AI tools, agents and automations
- ten to one hundred informal channels and group chats
- one hundred to one thousand live relationships with customers, partners and communities

You are still being told to treat them as a resource reporting into a line manager inside a business unit.

The physics do not match.

### 3.4 When Physics Break, Blame Follows The Wrong Line

When systems start to fail under new physics, institutions tend to do three things:
1. blame the people closest to the visible failure
2. increase pressure and oversight
3. demand better performance without changing structure

If you are a CEO, you know this pattern well. You have lived it.

You are not the cause. You are the pressure valve.

The more you try to fix a physics problem with personal effort, the more exhausted you become and the more your stakeholders misdiagnose the situation.

They see a performance issue.

What you are really facing is an architectural issue.

The organisation is wired for a world that no longer exists.

### 3.5 The Hybrid Society Lens

Hybrid society is a simple idea:

> Every meaningful action now takes place in a mixed environment of humans and AIs, each amplifying or distorting the other.

Your customers use AI to discover, compare, complain and coordinate.  
Your staff use AI to build, respond and improvise.  
Your regulators use AI to monitor, react and sometimes punish.  
Your competitors use AI to copy, undercut or outmanoeuvre you.

You are not running a company in isolation. You are participating in a dense web of human plus AI behaviour that you do not fully see.

Once you accept that this is the real game, the old CEO job description falls apart.

The good news is that something better replaces it.

### 3.6 From SWOT to CoOrbit – The End Of Flat Strategy

For most of your career, the default way to talk about strategy was a simple four box slide: SWOT.

Strengths. Weaknesses. Opportunities. Threats.

A neat grid; a comfortable workshop; a photo of a whiteboard that felt like progress.

The problem is not that SWOT was foolish. The problem is that it was flat.

It treated your organisation as if it lived on a calm surface, where a small set of internal and external factors could be summarised once a year and handed to the board.

Hybrid society does not live on a flat surface. It lives in a shifting field.

Staff use AI to change their own leverage each week. Customers use AI to change their expectations each day. Competitors use AI to copy and undercut you each hour.

You do not live on a SWOT grid. You live inside a moving field.

We call that field your **CoOrbit Map**.

Internally we shorten this to **COR**; you can treat the two labels as the same thing. SWOT is a flat comfort chart. CoOrbit is the real risk and opportunity field that your organisation actually moves through.

At a glance, your CoOrbit Map should help you see:
- which parts of your organisation are genuinely resilient
- where AI is already amplifying your people
- where AI is silently eroding your business model
- where your behaviour is improving society
- where your behaviour is quietly making things worse

CoOrbit is not another workshop toy. It is the steward era successor to SWOT.

You will not master it in a single sitting. You will refine it as your organisation learns.

The important point is this:

> Stop pretending a flat four box grid can describe a three dimensional, AI powered world.

A Chief Steward Officer does not ask for a SWOT deck. A steward asks for a living CoOrbit Map and for the people who are brave enough to update it.

      The Legacy CEO role is being automated. If your edge sits inside vendor UIs and roadmaps, you’re renting learning speed. Steward CEOs keep their differentiators in their own repos and learn weekly from real usage.

      Decision-making: Legacy leaders centralize choices and push them down a hierarchy; Steward leaders network decisions - delegating authority to the edge and pulling evidence back weekly.

      Automation is moving up the stack. In the last era, advantage came from operating the same tools more efficiently. In the AI era, advantage comes from how quickly you can learn from real usage and update direction. “Legacy CEO” here means a leader whose decisions are centralized and timed to quarterly planning. “Steward” means a leader who delegates decisions to the edge and insists on weekly evidence.

      Legacy CEO (CEO/L)

      • Buys solution, then implements
      • Roadmap follows vendor cadence
      • Artifacts trapped in UI/DB
      • Evidence = slideware & anecdotes

      Steward CEO (“Steward”)

      • Assembles differentiators in own repos
      • Ships a usable slice weekly, pivots monthly
      • File-based prompts/flows/evals are portable
      • Evidence = shared scoreboard & guardrails

      Then: Giant strategy firm → giant monolithic solution vendor that owns Systems Integration. Scale came from sameness; customizations lived as tickets and bolt-ons.

      Now: Coevolution. Use independent strategy boutiques, independent Systems Integrators, and multi-vendor best practices before ingress of giant influencers. Small teams assemble from modular pieces, keep artifacts in their own repos, and learn weekly from real usage.

      Big consultancies and single-vendor stacks once delivered resilience through sameness. That broke as cycles sped up: the roadmap you buy lags the reality you face. The modern pattern is to keep your differentiators - prompts, tools, flows, evals - in your repos, connect them with thin adapters, and let teams expose small, usable slices weekly so evidence compounds.

      “Assemble vs buy” is not anti-vendor. Buy commodities (hosting, auth, logging, base models). Assemble the things that make you unique. Keep artifacts portable so switching is possible. The test is simple: can you list the files that define your edge and move them?

      • Adaptation gap: vendor roadmaps lag your pivots; competitors learn faster.
      • Lock-in drag: differentiators end up inside someone else’s UI/DB.
      • Eval blindness: anti-benchmark terms or shallow metrics hide quality issues.
      • Cost gravity: change orders & license floors outpace value shipped.

      CEO weekly tick-boxes

      • Two priority workflows selected
      • One usable slice shipped
      • Adoption KPI tracked (e.g., WAU/MAU of new capability)
      • Lead time & rework % trend reviewed
      • Guardrail gate reviewed; incidents closed with “efficiency wisdom” captured
      • Scoreboard updated; pivot note written

      Board governance tick-boxes

      • Evidence over claims (shared scoreboard)
      • Anti-lock-in stance (portable artifacts, exit plan)
      • TxO Steward / data boundaries in force
      • Benchmarking rights retained
      • Named artifacts on termination; vendor assistance SLAs

      A Steward cadence asks for two choices each week: which workflow to advance, and what constitutes a “usable slice.” Teams demo reality, not slides. You track adoption (WAU/MAU of the new capability), lead time, rework%, and decision quality. Incidents are expected; the point is to capture the “efficiency wisdom” and update guardrails.

      1. Pick 2–3 workflows; define a “usable slice” for week 1–2.
      2. Inventory commodities vs differentiators.
      3. Stand up adapters and evaluation harness.
      4. Run bake-off with at least two vendors; no anti-eval clauses.
      5. Keep artifacts portable; commit weekly.
      6. Write the exit plan before you sign.

      Solution sourcing starts with clarity: the first two workflows and the week-1/2 slice. Stand up evaluation harnesses and adapters early so bake-offs are honest. Require benchmarking rights, no anti-evaluation terms, and name the portability/exit plan before signature.

      • Adoption: WAU/MAU of the new capability
      • Lead time: idea → usable slice
      • Rework %: changes after exposure to users
      • Decision quality: % decisions backed by evidence

      The scoreboard focuses on leading indicators that move before revenue and margin do. WAU/MAU tell you whether anyone actually uses the new capability. Lead time tracks idea → usable slice. Rework % shows how much you changed after putting it in front of users (learning is good - rework should trend down as understanding improves). Decision quality is the percent of material decisions backed by evidence, not opinion.

      Review these weekly. If adoption stalls, ask: is the slice truly usable, or are guardrails too tight/loose? If lead time slips, remove handoffs. If rework spikes, celebrate learning but tighten evaluation harnesses.

      After 4–12 weeks of weekly slices, gather leadership to ask: what improved, what regressed, what to stop, what to double down on. Use the scoreboard, incident “efficiency wisdom,” and user proof.

      Product: plan by workflows not features. Ship usable slices weekly; treat prompts, tools, flows and evals as first-class artifacts. Roadmaps reference evidence, not vendor slides.

      Operations: move from ticket queues to instrumented flows. Track lead time and rework %, retire steps that add latency without raising quality. Incident reviews capture “efficiency wisdom.”

      Engineering: use adapters to keep options open. Prefer portable file formats for prompts/flows/evals. Bake evals into CI to catch regressions.

      Finance: measure learning-per-dollar and time-to-slice. Contracts name exit rights and artifact portability; licenses scale with adoption, not seats alone.

      Legal & Risk: codify TxO Steward gates, benchmarking rights, and data boundaries. Ban anti-evaluation clauses.

      People: steward craft, not compliance. Upskill PMs and leads to write specs as tests and evals. Celebrate evidence, not heroics.

      A TxO Steward (Transition Office Steward) is a short-term facilitator who sets the cadence, gates, and scoreboards that let a Steward organization learn weekly. They do not hold executive authority and are never presented as a co-CEO. Typical arc: week 0–1 CoAudit; weeks 1–4 slices; weeks 5–12 scale or stop; CoMirror after 4–12 weeks.

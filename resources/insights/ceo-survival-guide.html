    The CEO/L role is being automated. If your edge sits inside vendor UIs and roadmaps, you‚Äôre renting learning speed. Steward CEOs keep their differentiators in their own repos and learn weekly from real usage.

    3) How sourcing used to work ‚Äî and why it breaks now

    Then: Giant strategy firm ‚Üí giant monolithic solution vendor that owns Systems Integration. Scale came from sameness; customizations lived as tickets and bolt-ons.

    Now: Coevolution. Use independent strategy boutiques, independent Systems Integrators, and multi-vendor best practices before ingress of giant influencers. Small teams assemble from modular pieces, keep artifacts in their own repos, and learn weekly from real usage.

    12) Short FAQ

    Is this anti-vendor? No. Buy commodities from vendors you trust. Assemble your edge where you can move fast.

    Why weekly slices? Real usage beats decks. It de-risks spend and accelerates learning.

    Can this coexist with big firms? Yes. Use a pre-SI boutique first. Bring in large firms after the approach is proven.

    13) What changes by function

    Product: plan by workflows not features. Ship usable slices weekly; treat prompts, tools, flows and evals as first-class artifacts. Roadmaps reference evidence, not vendor slides.

    Operations: move from ticket queues to instrumented flows. Track lead time and rework %, retire steps that add latency without raising quality. Incident reviews capture ‚Äúefficiency wisdom.‚Äù

    Engineering: use adapters to keep options open. Prefer portable file formats for prompts/flows/evals. Bake evals into CI to catch regressions.

    Finance: measure learning-per-dollar and time-to-slice. Contracts name exit rights and artifact portability; licenses scale with adoption, not seats alone.

    Legal & Risk: codify TxO Steward gates, benchmarking rights, and data boundaries. Ban anti-evaluation clauses.

    People: steward craft, not compliance. Upskill PMs and leads to write specs as tests and evals. Celebrate evidence, not heroics.

    14) Industry sketches (portable patterns)

    Telecom & Datacoms: ticket triage + doc agents reduce lead time from weeks to days; adapters prevent vendor lock-in as networks modernize.

    Healthcare: intake, prior auth, and discharge summaries become evidence loops; privacy gates and audit trails are part of the slice, not an afterthought.

    Financial Services: decisioning agents run inside guardrails; evaluators catch drift before it hits customers; portability defends against pricing shocks.

    Industrial: SOPs become executable checklists; on-device assist + offline fallbacks; human overrides logged as learnable signals.

    Public Sector & Education: transparent scoreboards build trust; bias/impact tests live with the code; adoption gates align with policy cycles.

    15) Common risks & anti-patterns

    Deck-driven programs: evidence arrives last. Flip it: show slices first; then talk slideware.

    One-vendor capture: differentiators trapped in someone else‚Äôs UI/DB. Keep artifacts in your repos with slim adapters.

    Shadow pilots: half-owned experiments with no adoption path. Every slice names its eventual owner and KPI before week one.

    ‚ÄúCo-CEO‚Äù posturing: the TxO Steward is a facilitator, not a rival executive. Language matters; so does time-box.

    16) Cadence: the weekly evidence loop

    Pick two workflows, ship one usable slice weekly, review adoption and rework %, and write a one-paragraph pivot note. Share a visible scoreboard so decisions compound.

    Board oversight moves from annual surveys to the same scoreboard. The aim is not certainty; it‚Äôs a faster, safer learning rate.

    17) Economics that matter

    Time-to-slice: idea ‚Üí first usable slice. Adoption velocity: WAU/MAU of the new capability. Rework %: changes after exposure. Decision quality: % backed by evidence. These are leading indicators of margin and resilience.

    18) Data, safety, and auditability

    Design gates where data flows, not where org charts sit. Keep a changelog of prompts/tools/flows; evaluate accuracy, bias, and impact routinely; log human overrides as learn-signals. Make audits boring by being consistent.

    19) The TxO Steward engagement (time-boxed)

    A TxO Steward (Transition Office Steward) is a short-term facilitator who sets the cadence, gates, and scoreboards that let a Steward organization learn weekly. They do not hold executive authority and are never presented as a co-CEO. Typical arc: week 0‚Äì1 CoAudit; weeks 1‚Äì4 slices; weeks 5‚Äì12 scale or stop; CoMirror after 4‚Äì12 weeks.

    20) Appendix: quick references

    Assemble vs Buy: buy commodities (infra, auth, logging, base models); assemble your differentiators (prompts, tools, flows, evals) in your repos with vendor-agnostic adapters.

    Scoreboard examples: Adoption, Lead time, Rework %, Decision quality.

    Legal guardrails: benchmarking rights, no anti-eval terms, named artifacts on termination, vendor assistance SLAs.
